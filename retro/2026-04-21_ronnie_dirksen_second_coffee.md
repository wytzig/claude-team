# A Second Coffee — In Twelve Parts
*Ronnie Rietman & Dirksen. Café De Zwarte Brug. The following evening. The fog had not fully lifted.*

---

**I.**
They hadn't planned a second night. It was the kind of thing that happens without being decided — the day ended, the option presented itself, and neither of them had a better reason to be somewhere else. Dirksen arrived first. He had his phone out when Ronnie came through the door, which was unusual; Dirksen usually put his phone away in company. Ronnie registered this and said nothing yet.

**II.**
The owner poured without ceremony. The café had the same quality it always had at this hour: the sense of a place that had outlasted its own relevance and found a kind of dignity in it. Two coffees. Same table. Outside, the fog from the night before had not fully lifted — it had thinned to a grey gauze over the canal, the kind of atmosphere that makes the city look like something in the process of being remembered rather than experienced.

**III.**
Dirksen turned his phone face-up on the table. Not as a gesture of sharing, exactly — more as an acknowledgment. He had been carrying the thing since midday and wanted it out of his hands, at least in principle. The headline was visible. Ronnie read it without picking up the device. *Onverklaard licht boven drie steden. Wetenschap zwijgt.* Unexplained light over three cities. Science is silent.

**IV.**
It had started the previous night. Rotterdam, Amsterdam, Ghent — almost simultaneously, around twenty-three hundred hours. Not meteorological. Not military aircraft, according to military sources, which was itself a sentence worth turning over. The accounts described a sustained amber glow above the cloud layer, stationary for eleven minutes, then gone. No sound. Photographs existed but were inconclusive in the way that photographs of unusual things always seem to be.

**V.**
"Amber," Ronnie said.

Dirksen looked at him.

"The color. Amber."

Dirksen set his spoon down slowly. They had both been under amber streetlights the night before, sitting in a café going cold at the edges, and something in the word now carried a charge it hadn't had then. Neither of them was given to mysticism. Neither of them remarked on this.

**VI.**
The second thing was the technology announcements. Three companies — one Dutch logistics firm, one German industrial automation group, one consortium Ronnie had not heard of but recognized by its investors — had each issued statements that morning within four hours of each other, describing what they called *autonomous partnership frameworks*. New integration between robotics systems and what the press releases called next-generation adaptive intelligence. The language was careful in the way corporate language is careful when it is trying not to explain something.

**VII.**
"Gecoördineerd," Dirksen said. It was not a question.

"Or the same source," said Ronnie. "Or both."

They considered this. The café owner refilled their cups without being asked, which was a privilege that had taken years to earn and was never remarked upon.

**VIII.**
Where the money was was not immediately obvious. That was interesting in itself. Usually the commercial angle on a technology announcement is visible before the press release is finished — you read the words and you see the supply chain, the distribution contracts, the secondary markets. Here the angle was obscured. Which meant either the announcement was genuine and the implications hadn't settled yet, or the announcement was a shape thrown to make something else harder to see.

**IX.**
Ronnie named three people. He didn't name them aloud. He named them to himself, in the silence of a pause that looked, from the outside, like him simply drinking his coffee. Dirksen, who knew the rhythm of how Ronnie thought, understood that a list was being made. He wrote nothing down. Some things are safer in the space between two people who trust each other than in any system designed to hold information.

**X.**
By the time they reached for their coats, the sky above the canal was doing something neither of them could name. It was not the amber from the news. It was not fog. It was closer to the feeling of a screen in another room — a glow that suggests content without revealing it. Dirksen looked at it for a long moment. Ronnie looked too. Neither of them had anything useful to say about it, which was almost the same thing as saying something.

**XI.**
There is a kind of conversation that looks, from outside, like two men sitting in a café. It is not that. It is a system for processing the world that happens to require two specific people in a specific configuration — the right amount of trust, the right amount of difference, enough time. You could sit in their chairs and not reproduce what they had. The chairs were just chairs. The conversation was something else.

**XII.**
They left while the glow was still above the city. They shook hands at the corner, as they always did. The grip was slightly longer than usual — not deliberate, just what happened. On the dark web, at that moment, in channels neither of them would ever find, a small game was passing between hands. Twelve scenes. Two men. Cold April. It had arrived the way important things sometimes do: quietly, from somewhere unexpected, into places where people were already watching.
