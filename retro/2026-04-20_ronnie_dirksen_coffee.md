# The Late Coffee — A Retrospective in Twelve Parts
*Ronnie Rietman & Dirksen. Café De Zwarte Brug. Post-shift. The fog came in off the canal around half past ten.*

---

**I.**
The shift had ended the way good shifts end: not with celebration, but with the quiet satisfaction of work that held its shape. Dirksen had closed the hard one — the customer who'd been circling for three weeks, playing numbers, testing patience. He'd closed it the way he always did: not by pushing, but by making the alternative feel expensive. Ronnie had watched from across the room and said nothing. He didn't need to.

**II.**
They walked out together into the cold. The street was empty. The canal, two blocks east, sent fog rolling over the cobblestones in slow sheets, the way water moves when it has nowhere left to go. Dirksen turned up his collar. Ronnie pulled his hands into his pockets. Neither of them spoke for almost a full minute, which was, for both of them, a form of respect.

**III.**
The café was the kind of place that didn't try. No menu on a chalkboard, no music worth naming, two bare bulbs above the bar. The owner, an older man with the kind of face that had stopped expecting surprises, poured their coffees without being asked. Dirksen had been coming here long enough to be known. Ronnie had been coming here long enough to be trusted. There is a difference.

**IV.**
"That second customer," Ronnie said, wrapping both hands around the cup. He didn't finish the sentence. He didn't need to. They'd both felt it — the way the man had tilted his questions differently depending on which of them he was speaking to, as if running two separate negotiations in parallel. It was a technique. Not a bad one. Just visible, if you knew to look.

**V.**
Dirksen turned his spoon over once and set it down. "Wist je het meteen?" — Did you know right away? Ronnie considered this. The honest answer was no. The honest answer was that he'd felt something off in the body language before his mind had caught up with why. He said as much. Dirksen nodded. He had the same experience, mirrored — he'd known it intellectually first and felt it later, which was the way Dirksen usually moved through the world: head first, instinct confirming.

**VI.**
Outside, the fog had thickened. Through the café window, the streetlight had become a smear of amber, formless, as if the light itself had lost confidence. A tram passed somewhere in the distance — they heard it before it appeared and long after it vanished. The city was still doing its work, even now, even in the cold. They both found this reassuring without saying so.

**VII.**
They talked about what had worked. The rhythm of the day — how they'd moved between customers without stepping on each other, how Ronnie had stayed in the room when Dirksen needed silence to press, how Dirksen had re-entered a conversation Ronnie had warmed and brought it somewhere specific. This wasn't rehearsed. It had just grown over time, the way trust grows: through accumulated small proofs, each one unremarkable on its own.

**VIII.**
Dirksen ordered a second coffee and lit a cigarette he'd been turning over in his fingers for twenty minutes. He didn't usually smoke. Ronnie noted this but did not file it as a problem. Some nights are different without being worse. "Ik zat in mijn hoofd," Dirksen said. *I was in my head.* Ronnie said, "I noticed." Not as an accusation. As a receipt. As proof that being seen hadn't gone unregistered.

**IX.**
The conversation shifted, the way late-night conversations do — not by decision but by drift. They talked about a deal from six weeks ago that still sat wrong with Ronnie. Something about the terms, not the numbers but the tone, the way the other party had been too agreeable at the end. Dirksen thought he was overthinking it. Ronnie said maybe. Dirksen said the money had cleared. Ronnie said money wasn't the only thing that could clear.

**X.**
By eleven the café was nearly empty. The owner moved behind the bar with the unhurried efficiency of a man who has nothing left to prove. The fog outside was now total — the window showed nothing but the faint reflection of their own table, their own cups, themselves slightly ghosted. Dirksen looked at it for a long moment. "Je ziet ons bijna niet," he said quietly. *You can barely see us.* Ronnie didn't answer. He looked too. There was something in it — two men, in the glass, half-dissolved in fog.

**XI.**
The question Ronnie asked that night — the one Dirksen would write about in his log, *the right one* — was this: *"What would you have needed, earlier today, that you didn't ask for?"* It was not a therapeutic question. It was a practical one. Dirksen sat with it for long enough that the silence became its own answer. Then he said: a minute. Just a minute alone before the close. Not doubt, not nerves — just space. Ronnie said he'd give him that, next time. Dirksen believed him.

**XII.**
They left around half past eleven. The fog had not lifted and would not lift — it was the kind of cold April night that owns itself completely. They shook hands at the corner, which was how they always said goodbye: a short grip, direct, no theater. Their breath hung in the air a moment after they'd parted, two small clouds drifting apart above the cobblestones, then gone. The canal pulled the fog east. The streetlights held their amber posts. And the city, indifferent and faithful, kept its watch through the long dark of the night.
