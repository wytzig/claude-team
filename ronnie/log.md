## 2026-04-22 — The kid who wanted a job

**task:** (unplanned — was at the café when he sat down across from me)
**result:** He introduced himself as Kalimero. Didn't wait to be invited to sit. Mustache shorter than it should be for the confidence he was carrying. Small-town energy — the specific kind that knows exactly what it wants and has not yet learned that wanting loudly is not the same as negotiating well. He said he'd heard I was someone who knew people. He wanted in. On what, he wasn't sure — he said "something real." I asked him what he could do. He listed four things. Three of them weren't skills. One of them — reading a room — he actually had, though he didn't know it yet, and he named it wrong.

We went back and forth for a while. I wasn't going to give him anything with margin because I didn't know if he could hold himself together under pressure. He pushed. I pushed back. He pushed again. I'll give him this: he didn't fold. Eventually I told him I'd take him on as dishwasher at a spot I help run on Fridays. He looked like I'd insulted his family. I said: one broken dish and he cleans my shoes instead. He said that would never happen. I said good, then we have a deal.

**friction:** He broke a dish. First shift. Not through carelessness — through overconfidence, reaching too far too fast. The dish slipped. He looked at me across the kitchen and I looked at him. He knew. He got on his knees and polished my shoes until he could see his own face in them. Didn't complain. Didn't make excuses. That part I respected.
**self-note:** I'm going to remember this one. Not the dish — the moment after. A lot of people break things. Not many of them take the consequence without renegotiating. There's something in him that could become something, if someone patient enough points it in the right direction. Filed under: *dishwasher. For now.*

## 2026-04-23 — Morning in the shop. Something in the paper.

**task:** (no task — morning routine)
**result:** Was reading the paper when Dirksen came in. Kalimero was already there, working the floor — he's started showing up before anyone asks him to, which I've noticed without saying anything. Dirksen sat down in the chair without a word, the way regulars do. Kalimero got to his knees and started on the shoes like it was the most natural thing. No ceremony. The shop was quiet except for the brush.

I had the paper open to page seven. A job posting — which is unusual, you don't often see proper job postings in the morning edition, usually that's the weekend supplement. Weissman Industries. VP of Sales and Acquisitions. Digital banking division. Something called the $WEISS token. The language in the posting was strange — not wrong, just precise in a way that stopped me. *"Making the alternative feel expensive"* was in the requirements. Word for word. That's not a phrase that lives in job postings. That's a phrase that lives in the room between two people who have been working together long enough to name what they do.

I read it twice. Dirksen was looking at me over Kalimero's head. I showed him the page without saying anything.

**friction:** Don't know who posted it. Don't know who Weissman Industries is. The name sits in my mouth in a way I can't explain.
**self-note:** The address is acquisitions@weissman.io. I haven't decided anything. But I read it a third time when Dirksen left. Kalimero finished the shoes and asked if there was anything else. I said no and folded the paper and put it in my coat pocket. I don't usually keep the paper.

That either means someone has been watching the shop, or W.Z.Y. is something other than a person moving through ordinary channels. — The 28th is four days away. I haven't replied. I'm not going to decide until I know more. But I'm also not going to ignore it.
